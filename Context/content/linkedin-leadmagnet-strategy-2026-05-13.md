## Part 1: Lead Magnet Strategy

*Demand Gen brief: gated assets and tools that earn an email address and open a sales conversation with German decision-makers who are expanding into or operating in Eastern Europe.*

### Strategic Principle

German B2B buyers in the CCO/COO/VP-CX bracket are research-led before they are relationship-led. They do not fill in forms for generic "Ultimate Guides." They download tools that give them a specific number they need to justify a decision, or reports that contain intelligence they cannot easily produce themselves. Every lead magnet below is designed to produce a number, a benchmark, or a risk map — not marketing content dressed as insight.

The Option 2 angle is the lead magnet's natural differentiator: these assets are only relevant to someone who is already operating or actively planning operations in Eastern Europe. This self-selection is the qualification mechanism. Someone who downloads the Eastern Europe CX Cost Benchmarking Report is, by definition, thinking about CX delivery in Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, or Romania. That is an MQL.

**Format:** Interactive web-based calculator (embeddable on axendi.com, gated with email, LinkedIn lead gen form compatible)
**Language:** German and English versions
**Priority:** Immediate — build in Month 1

### Page Foundation

**Current challenge:** Axendi's LinkedIn company page likely reflects its Polish/OEX Group identity and existing market positioning. For the German market under Option 2, the page needs to speak directly to a German CCO or COO who lands on it after seeing an outbound message or ad, and immediately understands: "This company works with German companies in Eastern Europe. **Page bio direction (German-facing tagline):**

**About section structure:**
1. Opening sentence: State the German market problem directly. "Deutsche Unternehmen, die in Osteuropa wachsen, stehen vor einer gemeinsamen Herausforderung: exzellenten Kundenservice in polnisch, tschechisch, ungarisch und rumänisch zu liefern — ohne den Aufbau eigener Strukturen vor Ort."

5. CTA: "Sprechen Sie uns an" or link to CEE cost calculator.

**Cover image:** A visual that communicates "Eastern Europe + Germany + CX" — not a call center floor photo (generic). Consider: a map of Europe with Poland, Czech, Hungary, Romania, Germany highlighted, with a subtle "connecting" visual treatment. Or: a split visual — German corporate environment + Eastern European city. The image must communicate geography at a glance.

### Content Pillars

The company page should operate on four content pillars that collectively tell the Option 2 story without ever being a brochure.

**Pillar 1: Eastern Europe CX Intelligence (40% of posts)**
What it is: Data, observations, and insights about customer service delivery in Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Romania. What consumers in these markets expect. How CX differs from Germany. What regulations matter.

Examples:
- "Polish e-commerce consumers have a 72-hour return expectation — and 68% prefer WhatsApp for returns queries. Is your CX team equipped for this?"
- "The RODO (Polish GDPR equivalent) has one meaningful difference from the German DSGVO that affects how you structure your outsourcing contract. Most German legal teams miss it."
- "Average handle time in Czech B2C insurance claims: 4.2 minutes. In Germany: 6.8 minutes. Why the gap, and what it means for your SLA design."

Why: This pillar builds Axendi's credibility as the company that actually knows Eastern Europe CX from the inside. Competitors cannot post this content — they do not operate there at scale.

**Pillar 2: Client Results and Social Proof (25% of posts)**
What it is: Translated/adapted case study content from IKEA, Allegro, L'Oreal. Numbers. Outcomes. Real projects described with as much specificity as client confidentiality allows.

Examples:
- "For a major European retailer with operations in Poland and Germany, we reduced cost-per-contact by 31% in the first six months by deploying Primebot for returns queries. Here's how."
- "When a leading beauty brand entered the Polish market, they needed 120 Polish-speaking agents within 8 weeks. We delivered 127, fully trained, in Week 6."
- Milestone posts: "We now serve 1,700+ agents across [X] languages. But the number that matters most to our clients is this one: [key metric]."

**Pillar 3: AI + Automation in Multilingual CX (20% of posts)**
What it is: Primebot-led content. How AI automation works when your customer base speaks five languages. The technical and operational reality of multilingual chatbots and voicebots.

Examples:
- "Most AI chatbot vendors promise multilingual support. Here is what it actually means to automate a customer inquiry in Polish vs. German — and why the gap in NLU quality matters."
- "Primebot's deflection rate in Polish: 43%. In German: 47%. In Czech: 38%. What drives the difference, and how we close it."
- Broader AI in CX posts: thought leadership on automation replacing vs. augmenting agents, data on where AI fails in customer service and why.

**Pillar 4: The Germany-Eastern Europe Bridge (15% of posts)**
What it is: Directly spoken content to the German executive audience. Addressing their specific anxieties about operating in Eastern Europe. Making the bridge visible.

Examples:
- "5 things German companies underestimate about customer service in Poland. Number 3 will affect your CSAT before you notice it."
- "You've expanded into the Czech Republic. Your German CX team is trying to handle Czech customers. Here's what happens to your CSAT scores when you wait too long to localize."
- Content around German regulatory questions: "Can a German company legally outsource customer service to Poland? Yes. Here is exactly what your Auftragsverarbeitungsvertrag needs to cover."

---

### Posting Cadence and Format Mix

**Frequency:** 4–5 posts per week. Consistency over volume.

**Format mix (per week):**
- 2 x text posts with a single data point or insight (no image needed — algorithm favors these for reach when engagement rate is high)
- 1 x carousel (CEE country insight, how-to, or comparison — carousels generate the highest save rate on LinkedIn)
- 1 x client result / milestone post (image or short-form video if available)
- 1 x engagement post or question directed at the target audience (polls perform well for audience research: "Which Eastern European market is creating the most CX complexity for your team?" — the responses are MQL intelligence)

**Language strategy:**
- Primary language: German for all Pillar 1, 2, and 4 posts targeted at the German audience
- English for Pillar 3 (AI/tech content has broader reach in English and positions Axendi globally)
- Occasional Polish-language posts to reinforce native delivery credibility (German audience does not need to read them — the fact that Axendi posts in Polish signals authenticity)
- Avoid bilingual posts within the same update — choose the language for the audience, do not split.

**Tone and voice:**
- Direct and specific. German B2B decision-makers respond to precision, not inspirational language. "43% deflection rate" beats "transforming the future of customer service."
- Expert, not promotional. Axendi should sound like it knows Eastern Europe CX from the inside, not like it is selling a product.
- Formally professional with occasional informality in insight posts. Match the register of a senior operations executive writing on LinkedIn — not a marketing department.
- Never use superlatives without numbers. "Best-in-class" means nothing. "31% cost reduction in 6 months" means everything.

**Tone to actively avoid:**
- "We're excited to announce..." — sounds like a press release
- "Innovative solution for..." — generic BPO noise
- "Our passionate team..." — irrelevant to a CCO evaluating vendors

## Part 3: LinkedIn Personal Brand Strategy for Axendi's German Market Representatives

*Content Marketing and Demand Gen joint brief: how Axendi's sales reps, AEs, SDRs, and executives active in the German market should position their personal LinkedIn profiles to support the Option 2 positioning.*

### Why Personal Brands Matter More Than the Company Page

Research consistently shows personal profiles generate 8x more engagement than company pages on LinkedIn. German B2B buyers — particularly in the conservative, relationship-driven procurement culture of Germany — want to buy from people they trust before they buy from companies. The company page establishes that Axendi exists and is credible. The personal brand is what makes the CCO pick up a meeting request.

For a cold-start market with zero German logos, personal credibility is Axendi's substitute for local reference. The individual representing Axendi in Germany must be visibly knowledgeable about Eastern Europe CX, about the German buyer's challenges, and about the operational realities of cross-border CX outsourcing — before any sales conversation starts.

---

### Profile Positioning

**Headline (the most-viewed field after your name):**

Avoid: "Account Executive at Axendi" — communicates nothing.
Avoid: "Helping companies with BPO solutions" — generic.

Use instead: One of these frameworks:
- "[Title] | Helping German companies deliver CX in Poland, Czech Republic, and CEE | Axendi"
- "CX Outsourcing for Eastern Europe | Supporting German expansion teams | Axendi"
- "[Title] at Axendi | Multilingual Contact Center & AI Automation | German-CEE Market"

The headline should contain at minimum: (a) the geographic specificity (Eastern Europe / CEE), (b) a German-market signal, (c) Axendi. It should communicate expertise at a glance, not just a job title.

**About section structure:**

Paragraph 1 — The pain point: "Many German companies expanding into Poland, Czech Republic, or Hungary face the same problem: serving local customers in their native language, at German quality standards, without building a captive operation from scratch."

Paragraph 2 — Who they are and what they do: Write in first person. "I work with CCOs, COOs, and Operations leaders at German e-commerce and financial services companies who are either already operating in Eastern Europe or planning to enter. I help them build and run customer service operations that are multilingual, GDPR-compliant, and AI-augmented — without the overhead of building it themselves."

Paragraph 3 — Proof: Reference the Axendi capability specifically. "Through Axendi, I connect German clients to a delivery network of 1,700+ multilingual agents across CEE markets, backed by Primebot AI automation that handles 30–50% of contact volume without human intervention."

Paragraph 4 — CTA: "If you're looking at CEE CX and want to know what the cost and quality comparison actually looks like, I'm happy to run through our benchmarking data. Reach out or book 20 minutes here: [Calendly link]"

**Featured section:**
Pin three items:
1. The CEE CX Cost Calculator (link to gated tool — drives lead magnet conversions from profile visitors)
2. The most compelling company page post from Axendi (amplifies company page reach)
3. One personal thought leadership post with high engagement (social proof of expertise)

**Profile photo and banner:**
Professional headshot, neutral background. Banner image should reinforce the Axendi/CEE/Germany brand — can be the same visual language as the company page cover image, adapted for personal format.

### What Personal Brand Accounts Post

The company page and personal accounts must not post the same content. They serve different functions.

**Company page:** Institutional credibility. Data. Case studies. Brand-level thought leadership. Posts that an executive would share to a committee as background reading.

**Personal account:** Human insight. First-person observations. Opinions. War stories (anonymized). The things a CCO would share with a peer over dinner at CCW, not what they'd put in a board presentation.

**Personal post categories (rotate across these):**

Category 1 — Field observations (2–3 per month)
First-person experiences from the German-Eastern Europe CX space. What a sales conversation revealed about the market. What a prospect said that was surprising. What a client discovered in their CEE pilot that changed their assumptions. Keep it specific and real.

Examples:
- "Spoke with a Head of Operations at a German e-commerce company this week. They've been in Poland for 3 years. Their #1 complaint wasn't cost — it was escalation. When a Polish customer complaint escalates, there's no German speaker on the ground to make the call. Here's how we've solved this for similar situations."
- "Had a conversation yesterday where a CCO told me they'd built an in-house team of 12 Polish speakers in Frankfurt. Fully loaded cost: EUR 650K/year. Volume: 8,000 contacts/month. We ran the numbers. Here's what they found."

Category 2 — CEE market intelligence (1–2 per month)
Share an insight about Poland, Czech, Hungary, or Romania that the German executive audience would not know from their own market.

Examples:
- "Polish consumers are significantly more likely to contact customer service via chat than phone — opposite of the German preference. If your CX model is built around inbound call handling, your Polish NPS is already suffering."
- "One thing German companies often miss: Romanian public holidays don't align with German ones. Your contact center SLA needs to account for this or you're breaching it on 9 days a year."

Category 3 — Opinion and takes (1–2 per month)
Views on AI, outsourcing, the German market, industry developments. These generate the most discussion and the most inbound from target personas.

Examples:
- "Hot take: German companies are 18 months behind their UK and US counterparts in AI-assisted customer service. And the gap is widening. Here's why — and what it costs them."
- "I think the 'local provider preference' in German BPO procurement is a rational bias that's becoming an irrational one. Here's my argument."

Category 4 — Community engagement posts (1 per month)
Referencing a German event, congratulating a peer, sharing a reaction to something the industry is discussing. These build social capital with the target audience.

**What NOT to post:**
- Company promotions or announcements (let the company page handle that, reshare those)
- Generic "Top 5 tips for CX excellence" without a specific Eastern Europe / Germany angle
- Anything that sounds like a vendor pitch rather than a peer perspective

### Engagement Strategy in German Business Conversations

Posting is only half the personal brand equation. Commenting — on the right content, in the right way — is what builds the network that converts.

**Daily engagement habit (10–15 minutes per day):**
- Identify 5–7 target posts from German CCOs, VP CX professionals, COOs at e-commerce and fintech companies, and German industry media (Callcenter Profi, Contact Management Magazine)
- Leave specific, substantive comments — not "Great post!" but adding data, a different angle, or a follow-up question that extends the conversation
- Follow every German senior CX/Operations professional who posts about CEE expansion, outsourcing, AI in customer service, or German e-commerce growth
- Engage with posts from German industry associations: CCV, Bitkom, bevh (Bundesverband E-Commerce und Versandhandel)

**Commenting framework:**
Structure comments as: [Validate the premise] + [Add a specific data point or experience] + [Ask a question that opens dialogue]

Example: A German CCO posts about challenges scaling their operations into Eastern Europe.
Comment: "The scaling challenge you're describing is almost universal for German companies entering CEE — the language gap in escalation paths is usually what surprises people first. In our experience deploying multilingual teams in Poland and Czech, the trigger point is usually month 3–4 when volume spikes unexpectedly and the German-side team realizes they can't handle native-language exceptions. What's your current plan for overflow routing when Polish contacts escalate beyond your German team's capacity?"

This comment: builds credibility, demonstrates operational expertise, references the Option 2 positioning, and ends with a question that (a) is valuable for the prospect to answer and (b) opens a conversation.

"[Name] — came across your post on [specific topic] and it aligned exactly with what we see in German companies entering Eastern Europe. I work with companies in a similar position at Axendi. Happy to connect."

**After connection is accepted — the warm-up sequence before any pitch:**
1. Day 1–3: Like or comment on something they recently posted. No message.
2. Day 7–10: Send a direct message with a piece of value — a link to one of Axendi's lead magnets that is directly relevant to something they've posted or their company situation. No ask.
"[Name], based on what you wrote about [topic], I thought this benchmarking data might be useful — it covers CX cost comparisons across Poland, Czech, and Germany. Happy to walk through the numbers if you want to go deeper: [link]"
3. Day 14–21: If they engaged with the content (opened, replied, clicked), move to a meeting request. "Glad that was useful. I'd love to show you the live version of our cost model for your specific setup — takes 20 minutes. Worth a quick call?"

**SSI (Social Selling Index) target:** Each Axendi German-market rep should target an SSI of 70+ (LinkedIn's benchmark for social sellers hitting quota is significantly higher than the average of 40). This requires consistent posting, active connection building with the target persona set, and engagement in relevant communities.

### How Personal Brands and the Company Page Reinforce Each Other

**Amplification mechanics:**
- Every company page post is reshared by all Axendi German-market reps within 30–60 minutes of publication. This dramatically increases organic reach of company page content into the target audience's feeds.
- Every personal post that performs well (high engagement in first 2 hours) is reshared by the company page with a brief framing comment.
- Company page lead magnets are promoted in personal bios (Featured section) and referenced in personal posts.

**Content coordination (no duplication, no contradiction):**
- Weekly 15-minute content sync between all personal brand account holders and VP Marketing to align on what the company page is posting that week vs. what personal accounts will post.
- Company page: institutional proof, data, brand-level intelligence.
- Personal accounts: first-person perspective, opinions, field stories that humanize and make specific what the company page states abstractly.
- They should feel like two sides of the same conversation — the company page says "Axendi helps German companies with CEE CX," the personal accounts show what that looks like from the inside.

**Persona ownership of content pillars:**
Consider assigning each team member a primary content area that matches their expertise:
- SDR/BDR: CEE market observations and Eastern Europe CX curiosities (builds audience while prospecting)
- AE: Deal pattern insights, buying process observations, client journey stories (builds trust with CCOs)
- Executive/Country Lead: Strategic opinion and thought leadership (builds authority at C-suite level)

This prevents the personal accounts from competing with each other or with the company page, and creates a coherent, multi-voice brand presence in the German market.
